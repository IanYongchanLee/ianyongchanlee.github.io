Machine Learning Research

Predicting Crop Yield with
Climate Factors

County-level yield forecasting with interpretable ML to support climate-resilient agricultural decision-making.

01. Project Purpose

Climate volatility is increasing yield uncertainty, which makes planning difficult for producers, insurers, and policy makers. This project builds an interpretable machine learning framework to forecast corn, soybean, and wheat yields at the county level, using multi-dimensional climate signals (temperature, precipitation, solar radiation, CO₂, soil moisture).

Key question

“Which climate features (and which months) are most predictive of yield outcomes — and how stable are those signals across crops?”

02. Methodology

Data Integration & Harmonization

  • • Built a reproducible pipeline to assemble county-level panels across multiple climate dimensions.
  • • Standardized time resolution into a consistent monthly structure to align signals across variables.
  • • Noted practical data constraints (e.g., some gridded products produce repeated values across counties due to coarse spatial resolution).

Modeling Strategy (XGBoost)

  • • Trained separate models by crop to respect crop-specific phenology and climate sensitivity.
  • • Used cross-validation to evaluate generalization and reduce sensitivity to a single split.
  • • Optimized model behavior with a bias toward stable out-of-sample performance (not just training fit).

Explainability (TreeSHAP)

  • • Applied TreeSHAP to quantify each feature’s marginal contribution to predictions.
  • • Interpreted “month_feature” patterns (e.g., 5_Solar = May solar radiation) to connect signals to growing-season dynamics.
  • • Produced global importance (ranked features) and directional insights (high vs. low values pushing yield up/down).

03. Results & Insights

Corn

R²: 0.759

RMSE: 11.99
NRMSE: 6.35%

Soybean

R²: 0.810

RMSE: 4.12
NRMSE: 7.46%

Wheat

R²: 0.478

RMSE: 12.15
NRMSE: 27.77%

Interpretation Highlights

  • • Corn and soybean show tight predicted-vs-actual clustering (strong generalization), while wheat exhibits higher dispersion (weaker fit).
  • • SHAP reveals that yield is driven by seasonal patterns rather than a single static feature—month-level climate signals matter.
  • • The explainability layer turns the model into an “early-warning” system: you can monitor the months/features most responsible for large deviations.

04. Limitations & Next Steps

  • • Some climate inputs are spatially coarse, which can reduce county-level uniqueness and compress signal variance.
  • • Wheat’s lower performance suggests missing drivers (e.g., management practices, cultivar differences, or region-specific agronomics).
  • • Next steps: add agronomic controls, test region-stratified models, and introduce lagged/accumulated features aligned to crop growth stages.

Sports Analytics

EPL Athlete Performance
Optimization Model

Quantifying how age relates to Premier League productivity using a parsimonious multiple regression model.

01. Project Purpose

This project asks: Does a player’s age meaningfully affect season productivity in the EPL? Because “performance” is multi-dimensional, I defined a clean, rate-based outcome: non-penalty goals + assists per 90 mins (G+A-PK_90), which normalizes for playing time.

Why this matters

Clubs make age-sensitive decisions (contracts, transfers, rotation). A transparent model helps separate “age effect” from confounders like team context, role, and progression metrics.

02. Methodology

Data & Feature Set

  • • Sourced player statistics from FBref (StatsBomb-powered match tracking) for the 2023–24 season.
  • • Response variable: G+A-PK_90.
  • • Candidate predictors included: age, position, team, minutes/starts, cards, and progressive actions (PrgC/PrgP/PrgG), plus additional categorical structure.

Model Search & Selection Logic

  • • Built multiple candidate regression specifications (7 models) to balance fit and practicality.
  • • Selected the final model using 4 criteria: Adjusted R², test correlation (pred vs actual), MSE, and simplicity (parameter count).
  • • Chose Model 3 because it delivered competitive predictive power with fewer parameters than more complex alternatives.

Statistical Validation

  • • Used coefficient t-tests and an ANOVA table to confirm overall model usefulness.
  • • Checked multicollinearity with VIF (all well below typical concern thresholds).
  • • Reviewed residual diagnostics; normality/constant variance showed mild issues, but linearity and independence were acceptable for this modeling goal.

03. Results & Insights

Final model (Model 3)

The final specification predicts G+A-PK_90 using only four drivers: Team, Position, Age, and PrgG (progressive passes received / progression involvement metric).

Adjusted R²

0.4644

Test Correlation

0.588

0.037

What the model says

  • • Age has a statistically significant positive association with G+A-PK_90 when controlling for team, position, and PrgG (coef ≈ 0.00724).
  • • Team and position act as strong contextual controls—productivity is not “pure skill,” it’s also system + role.
  • • Low VIF values suggest the model’s signals are not redundant, making interpretation stable.

04. How to Use It

  • • Compare players within the same role/team context to isolate age-related shifts.
  • • Use PrgG as a “involvement” proxy to separate high-touch attackers from low-involvement roles.
  • • Extend: add interaction terms (Age×Pos), nonlinear splines, or quantile regression to capture heterogeneous aging curves.

Health & Food Analytics

Global Life Expectancy &
Nutritional Correlation

Identifying dietary patterns associated with longer life expectancy using multi-dimensional Tableau analytics.

01. Project Purpose

This analysis focuses on a practical question: Which food groups and nutrients are most closely associated with longer life expectancy? Rather than treating nutrition as a single number, I broke the problem into two layers: food groups (what people eat) and nutrients (what those foods provide).

Analytical framing

The goal is correlation-based insight (not causality): surfacing global patterns that are consistent across countries and regions, and then stress-testing those patterns by comparing top- and bottom-life-expectancy countries.

02. Methodology

Data Integration

  • • Merged FAO food availability (food groups + nutrient indicators) with World Bank health indicators.
  • • Joined primarily by country code and year, then used 2018–2022 averages to emphasize cross-country differences (life expectancy was relatively stable across the window).
  • • Introduced a continent dimension for high-level pattern discovery before drilling down to country-level relationships.

Visualization Plan (Tableau)

  • • Boxplot: continent distribution of life expectancy.
  • • Heatmap: within-continent food-group consumption contrasts (what dominates inside each region).
  • • Scatterplots: nutrient vs life expectancy (energy + protein).
  • • Bar + radar: top-5 vs bottom-5 life expectancy countries to expose dietary and micronutrient gaps.

03. Results & Insights

Nutrients: Energy & Protein

  • • Both energy supply (kcal/day) and protein supply (g/day) show moderate positive correlation with life expectancy.
  • • Trendline interpretation: +10 kcal/day aligns with roughly +1.6 years life expectancy, and +1g protein/day aligns with ~+3.5 years (correlation, not causation).
  • • Each scatterplot shows R² ≈ 0.5, meaning these nutrients explain about half of cross-country variance in life expectancy.

Food Groups: The “Fish Gap”

  • • When comparing the top-5 vs bottom-5 life expectancy countries, fish & shellfish supply shows the most pronounced gap.
  • • A focused nutrient breakdown indicates the top group receives substantially more DHA, EPA, and healthy fatty-acid profiles (MUFA/PUFA/SFA)—largely sourced from fish & shellfish.

Regional Pattern

At the continent level, regions with higher life expectancy (notably Europe and parts of Asia) show consistently higher relative consumption of milk/dairy and vegetables within their own regional profiles—suggesting these groups often co-occur with stronger longevity outcomes.

04. Recommendations

  • • Increase consumption of fish & shellfish as a nutrient-dense lever (omega-3 rich profile: DHA/EPA).
  • • Include more milk/dairy and vegetables to mirror common patterns in higher-life-expectancy regions.
  • • Ensure sufficient baseline energy and protein intake daily as foundational drivers in the correlation analysis.

NYC Large Office Carbon Inventory &
LL97 Compliance Risk Benchmark

Built a reporting-grade Scope 1–2 inventory for NYC large offices (≥200k ft²), benchmarked a pseudo-portfolio against the market, and translated emissions into LL97 penalty exposure across future compliance periods.

Dashboard 1: Pseudo-Portfolio Baseline (CY 2024)

Dashboard 2: NYC Large Office Benchmark (Rockefeller vs Market)

01. Project Purpose

This project builds a reporting-grade Scope 1–2 emissions inventory for NYC large office buildings (GFA ≥ 200,000 ft²) using benchmarking disclosures, then connects emissions performance to Local Law 97 compliance risk. I created a pseudo-corporate portfolio centered on :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0} and benchmarked it against the NYC large-office market to support prioritization and compliance-risk storytelling.

Business framing

Move beyond “emissions reporting” into decision support: identify outliers, contextualize performance vs peers, and translate operational intensity into forward-looking regulatory cost exposure.

02. Methodology

Data Quality Rules & Audit-Ready Metrics

  • • Reclassified zero-emission records as missing/invalid disclosures (not “true zero”) to prevent downward bias.
  • • Tracked coverage-aware KPIs (e.g., “covered buildings only”) to keep YoY comparisons defensible.

KPI Construction & Standardization

  • • Produced consistent reporting metrics: Total emissions (tCO₂e), Intensity (kgCO₂e/ft²), and portfolio-weighted intensity (Σ emissions / Σ GFA).
  • • Maintained unit clarity to ensure cross-asset and peer comparability (especially for large-GFA outliers).

Portfolio + Market Benchmarking (Tableau)

  • • Dashboard 1: portfolio baseline — asset map, building summary, Scope/energy-source composition, YoY context.
  • • Dashboard 2: market benchmark — borough overview, intensity distribution, Rockefeller vs market, scale vs intensity positioning.

LL97 Compliance Risk Translation

  • • Estimated penalty exposure using transparent assumptions: exceedance × $268 / tCO₂e.
  • • Visualized risk across future compliance periods (2030s–2040s) under a “constant 2024 emissions” baseline to show the magnitude of regulatory tightening.

03. Results & Insights

Portfolio Drivers & Asset Prioritization

  • • Portfolio emissions are largely Scope 2-driven (electricity/steam), indicating grid and electrification levers matter most.
  • • Performance varies materially at the asset level; a few high-intensity buildings drive disproportionate risk—supporting targeted retrofits vs one-size-fits-all actions.

Financial Translation (LL97)

Quantified how maintaining current emissions can translate into material LL97 penalty exposure across future compliance periods, reframing decarbonization as both an environmental and financial decision.

04. Deliverables

  • • Scope 1–2 inventory (tCO₂e) and intensity metrics (kgCO₂e/ft²) with coverage-aware rules.
  • • Two Tableau dashboards: Portfolio Baseline + NYC Market Benchmark.
  • • LL97 penalty exposure views across multiple future compliance periods with transparent assumptions.
